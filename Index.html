Energetic Boundaries for Sensitive People

A short excerpt from an October 2018 essay by Kathleen Murphy.

If you’ve ever walked into a room and felt tired before you’ve said a single word, you already understand energetic boundaries. Your system is reading the space long before your mind catches up.

Most highly sensitive people try to solve this by shrinking: avoiding conflict, absorbing other people’s moods, and calling it “being kind.” In reality, it’s often self-abandonment in spiritual clothing.

A true boundary doesn’t push anyone away. It simply says, “This is my field, and I’ll tend to it with care.” From that place, you can love people freely without letting their chaos set your nervous system on fire.

Small Morning Rituals, Big Subtle Shifts

From a March 2018 reflection on simple daily practices.

The internet will tell you that you need a 20-step morning routine to become your “highest self.” What your soul usually wants is something far quieter and more honest: one small anchor that says, “I matter to myself today.”

One cup of tea sipped without your phone. One page in a notebook where you tell the truth. One slow breath before you open your email. These are not productivity hacks; they’re tiny acts of metaphysical hygiene. They clear the static so your own inner voice can find you again.

Listening to the Quiet Body

From an August 2017 essay on body wisdom and gentle attention.

The body whispers long before it raises its voice. A tight jaw that shows up every Sunday night. A flutter in the stomach when you say “yes” but mean “no.” A sudden exhale when you finally tell the truth.

Metaphysical wellness starts with taking these tiny shifts seriously. Not as medical diagnoses, but as spiritual post-it notes: “Something here needs your kindness.” When you meet your body with curiosity instead of criticism, it stops having to shout to be heard.

About Kathleen

Kathleen Murphy is a metaphysical wellness specialist and writer who blends grounded, everyday language with a deep respect for subtle energy and spiritual practice. Her work is less about “fixing” people and more about helping them remember what was never broken.

Since 2017, Kathleen has shared short essays and talks on inner alignment, compassionate boundaries, and the art of listening to your own nervous system as a guide. Her approach is gentle, practical, and quietly rebellious: no harsh self-improvement, no spiritual performance, just honest tools for sensitive humans living real lives.

Off the page, Kathleen is a quiet observer, lover of old books, and frequent walker of tree-lined city streets. Much of her work is shaped by conversations with clients and friends who are learning how to stay soft without losing themselves.

This site collects a small selection of her essays from 2017–2019. New work is announced first through her low-volume email list.
